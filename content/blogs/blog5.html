In inferential statistics, the null hypothesis is a general statement or default position that there is no difference between two measured phenomena or that two samples derive from the same general population. Here, we will explore whether the mean IMDB rating for Steven Spielberg and Tim Burton are the same or not.

Null and Alternative hypothesis

H0: Mean IMDB rating for Steven Spielberg and Tim Burton are NOT significantly different.

H1: Mean IMDB rating for Steven Spielberg and Tim Burton are significantly different.

Ratings on Steven Spielberg’s Films with confidence intervals

#creating the new data set
CI_steve <- movies %>%
  filter(director=="Steven Spielberg") %>% 
  summarise(mean_rating = mean(rating),
            sd_rating = sd(rating),
            count = n(),
            se_rating = sd_rating/sqrt(count),
            t_critical = qt(0.975, count-1), 
            margin_of_error = t_critical * se_rating,
            lower = mean_rating - t_critical * se_rating,
            upper = mean_rating + t_critical * se_rating
            )%>% 
 add_column(name="Steven Spielberg")

#t.test to get CI Steve
steve_ratings <- movies %>%
  filter(director=="Steven Spielberg") %>% 
  select(rating,director)

t.test(steve_ratings$rating, data=steve_ratings)
## 
##  One Sample t-test
## 
## data:  steve_ratings$rating
## t = 52, df = 22, p-value <2e-16
## alternative hypothesis: true mean is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  7.27 7.87

## mean of x 
##      7.57
